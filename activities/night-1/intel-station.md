Here are some high-level concepts for your 15-minute "Intel Station" software game, designed to feel like a real IMF database terminal.

### Platform Recommendation
**A Web App (Optimized for Tablets or Laptops)**
A responsive web app running in full-screen mode on a tablet or laptop is ideal. It requires essentially zero setup on the actual devices, feels exactly like a stationary "Intel Terminal," and makes integrating a backend AI API (like OpenAI or Gemini) straightforward without dealing with app store approvals.

### Core Interface Concept
A split-screen terminal. 
*   **The Left Panel ("Comms Link"):** A chat interface where kids communicate with the "IMF Central AI" (or a character like Luther).
*   **The Right Panel ("Data Viewer"):** A dynamic display area where unlocked intercepted images, audio files, or blueprints appear.

### The AI Mechanic
The AI agent is given a strict system prompt sequence. It acts as a "Gatekeeper." It is told the current state of the kids' knowledge based on the Briefing and will only release the next piece of intel if the kids ask a question containing a specific keyword or concept they just learned. This forces them to read, comprehend, and ask logical follow-up questions.

### Progression Flow (The 15-Minute Loop)

**Phase 1: What is The Light?**
*   *Action:* Kids ask the AI about "The Light" based on the briefing.
*   *AI Response:* The AI pulls up intercepted messages from "The Architect." The messages talk about "compiling code" and "digital perfection."
*   *Discovery:* The AI helps explain that The Light isn't a magical glowing rock; it's **software** (a powerful computer program or secret digital recipe). 

**Phase 2: Where is it?**
*   *Action:* Since it's software, the kids must ask where it is stored. 
*   *AI Response:* The AI searches for massive energy signatures needed to hold such software and unlocks a blueprint of a building.
*   *Discovery:* It's held in a **High-Security Server Vault**. (The AI explains a server vault is like a massive, highly armored library for computers instead of books).

**Phase 3: How is it protected?**
*   *Action:* The kids ask about security, defenses, or alarms at the vault.
*   *AI Response:* The AI decrypts the final security protocol file. A video or flashing diagram appears in the Data Viewer.
*   *Discovery:* The vault has a **Pressure-Sensitive Floor Grid**. Anyone entering must step only on pre-designated safe squares, or an alarm will trigger. (Depending on the night's story script, this perfectly transitions into the kids playing the physical "Pressure Steps" game).

### Difficulty Levels

**Junior Agents (Ages 4-8: "Guided Interface")**
*   **Input:** Text-to-speech (microphone button) or pre-generated clickable prompt bubbles (e.g., "What is a server?"). 
*   **Content:** Heavy on visuals and audio. When they unlock the "Software" clue, the terminal shows a cartoon animation of blocky code going into a giant computer. The AI uses simple analogies ("Software is like invisible instructions that tell a computer how to think").
*   **Puzzles:** Mostly matching shapes or colors on the terminal to authorize the AI to search.

**Senior Agents (Ages 9-12: "Command Line Interface")**
*   **Input:** Physical typing. They have to formulate their own questions based on the clues. 
*   **Content:** Text-heavy intercepted emails or encrypted audio files. The AI behaves more rigidly, requiring them to use exact phrasing or logical deductions ("I cannot search for the location until you decipher what format 'The Light' is stored in").
*   **Puzzles:** Simple logic gates or pattern recognition to "bypass firewalls" before the AI is allowed to answer their questions.
